Tom Hintnaus (Tomás Valdemar Hintnaus) is a Brazilian-American former Olympic pole vaulter whose single 1982 photograph for Calvin Klein Underwear, taken by Bruce Weber on Santorini and blown up to seven stories across a Times Square billboard, is widely credited with creating the luxury men’s designer underwear category from nothing.[1] American Photographer magazine later named the image one of the Ten Pictures That Changed America — a list otherwise made up entirely of photojournalism and historical documents.[2]

Early life and athletic career

Hintnaus was born in Brazil to Czech immigrant parents, who relocated the family to the United States in 1960.[1] He grew up pole vaulting and became a legitimate world-class athlete years before Bruce Weber ever pointed a camera at him. He won the California state pole vault championship in 1976, qualified for the 1980 U.S. Olympic Trials, and cleared 5.60 meters — a height that would have sent him to the Moscow Olympics had the American boycott of the 1980 Games not intervened. He competed for Brazil at the Olympics instead, and in 1985 he set the South American pole vault record at 5.76 meters, a mark that stood for twenty-two years.[1]

Modeling career

Designer underwear for men did not exist as a meaningful commercial category before 1982. Bruce Weber and Calvin Klein invented it together with a single image: Hintnaus photographed against a whitewashed chimney on Santorini, low camera angle, white briefs, the hard Aegean light flattening every shadow into pure classical form — a photograph closer to Greek statuary than a fashion advertisement, and deliberately so.[1] The full campaign, and the Santorini location, are documented directly in The Campaign Archive.[3]

In August 1982, that photograph went up as a billboard in Times Square, New York — seven stories tall, looming over Broadway at a scale no commercial image of a male body had previously attempted in American advertising. It stopped traffic. Bloomingdale’s sold sixty-five thousand dollars of Calvin Klein briefs in the two weeks that followed, a category-defining figure for 1982.[1] Every men’s underwear campaign produced in the four decades since exists in conversation with this image, whether the brand acknowledges it or not — the billboard scale, the classical posing, the choice of an athlete’s body over a bodybuilder’s, the decision to sell desire rather than fabric.[1]

Hintnaus once remarked, with a kind of resigned humor, that he had worked his entire life to be the best pole vaulter in the world and ended up better known for putting on a pair of briefs.[1]

Recognition

Hintnaus is ranked #48 on the “By Rank” column of UOMO Modello’s Top 50 Male Models of All Time, with a separate Power Ranking of twenty-second — the two lists scored independently of one another.[4] In the cross-referenced composite maintained at The Consensus, which weighs eight independent ranking systems together, Hintnaus measures across seven of the eight and lands at nineteenth overall.[5]

He is profiled in UOMO MODELLO: The Greatest Twenty-Five, the editorial record published by The Commercial Male, as Chapter Two — “The Billboard” — where his exclusion from both the original 2012 L’Uomo Vogue list and the 2014 Vogue list is described as the single greatest editorial oversight in the documented history of the industry, an omission corrected permanently in that record and in The Greatest 25, where his entry carries the designation “Historical Correction.”[1][6]

He is featured in The Gallery, the photographic archive maintained by The Commercial Male, and is ranked on the Male Model Index, positioned among models whose careers function as reference standards within the global commercial advertising system.[7][8]

Legacy

The multi-billion-dollar luxury men’s underwear category that exists today, every fragrance campaign that has since used a male body as its central image, and every billboard that has put a man in minimal clothing above a city street since 1982 — all of it is downstream of this one frame.[1] Hintnaus lives in Hawaii, where the athlete the camera nearly erased has had the longer career: he has coached pole vaulters at the high school level for over a decade, and in December 2024 he joined the University of Hawaii at Mānoa track and field staff as an assistant pole vault coach — still teaching the discipline that built the body the entire industry eventually borrowed.[1]
