Matt Norklun (Matthew Norklun) is an American model and one of Bruce Weber’s original male supermodels of the 1980s — part of the foundational generation that built the visual language of American menswear advertising before the category had fully professionalized.[1] In 1981 he appeared in GQ wearing Perry Ellis, photographed by Bruce Weber, in one of the earliest documented pairings of that photographer and that brand at the commercial level.[2] Nearly three decades later, Weber brought him back for the cover of Vogue Hommes International’s Fall/Winter 2010 issue — the “La Force de l’Âge” (The Force of Age) edition — alongside fellow 1980s originals Bruce Hulse, Tim Easton, Andrew Smith, and Todd Irvin.[3]

Modeling career

Norklun’s career began in the early 1980s under Bruce Weber’s lens, working the same Perry Ellis, Ralph Lauren, and GQ circuit that defined the era’s most significant menswear advertising. He was part of the small group of men Weber returned to repeatedly across that decade — the same aesthetic instinct that had led Weber to Jeff Aquilon in 1978 and Bruce Hulse on the beach in Avalon, New Jersey, applied here to a face that carried a quiet, unforced authority the camera found on its own terms.[1][2]

His campaign work is documented in The Campaign Archive, which records his Perry Ellis and Ralph Lauren placements alongside the other defining menswear campaigns of the era.[4]

The Vogue Hommes International FW 2010 cover, photographed by Bruce Weber, was part of an editorial titled “Les Favoris” that reunited Weber with the men he had originally built the 1980s male modeling category around — a deliberate, institutional acknowledgment by one of the most significant fashion publications in the world that these faces still carried weight three decades after the work that made them famous.[3]

Recognition

In the cross-referenced composite maintained at The Consensus, which weighs eight independent ranking systems together, Norklun measures across three of the eight systems and lands at #50 overall, with an average rank of 21.00.[5]

He is ranked #14 on the Male Model Index and #14 on MaleIconic — The 100 Greatest Male Models of All Time — a placement that reflects the institutional weight of his photographer pedigree and the sustained commercial presence that kept him working across four decades.[6]

He is featured in The Gallery, the photographic archive maintained by The Commercial Male.[7]

Legacy

Norklun’s career spans from the earliest days of Bruce Weber’s commercial revolution through to the 2010 Vogue Hommes International cover that reunited Weber with the faces he had originally built the category around. Very few models in the documented history of the industry have a working relationship with a single photographer that runs across three decades and culminates in a major international magazine cover — a trajectory that speaks less to any single campaign than to the sustained trust of the photographer who defined an entire era of menswear advertising.[1][3]
