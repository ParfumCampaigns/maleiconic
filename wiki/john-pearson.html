John Pearson (born c. 1966) is a British model widely credited as the world’s first male supermodel. Discovered at eighteen while managing a denim shop in Hull, Yorkshire, he relocated to New York in 1986 and became the most sought-after male model of the 1990s, appearing as the face of Calvin Klein, Ralph Lauren, Donna Karan, Valentino, Burberry, Levi’s, and Gap, among others. He is the only man to have featured editorially in American GQ for more than three decades.[1]

Early life

Pearson grew up in Sheffield and Hull, in the north of England. At eighteen, while working as a manager of a denim shop, a photographer walked in and asked to take his picture — an encounter Pearson has described as entirely unexpected, and the moment that redirected his life.[2] Three years later, in 1986, he moved to New York, arriving at the precise moment the supermodel era was taking shape. Within days of landing he had shot the cover of Self magazine alongside Uma Thurman.[3]

Modeling career

Over the following decades, Pearson became a fixture in the upper tier of commercial and editorial fashion. His client list included Calvin Klein, Ralph Lauren, Donna Karan, Valentino, Burberry, Levi’s, Gap, Banana Republic, and Guy Laroche, and he appeared in more than 100 commercials. On the editorial side, he worked across Vogue Italia, L’Uomo Vogue, Vogue Hommes, and VMAN, and maintained a continuous presence in American GQ for over thirty years — a record unique among male models.[1][4]

The photographers he worked with read as a catalogue of the era’s greatest image-makers: Herb Ritts, Bruce Weber, Helmut Newton, Peter Lindbergh, Mario Testino, Patrick Demarchelier, Steven Meisel, David Bailey, Arthur Elgort, and Ellen von Unwerth, among others. Of Pearson, Herb Ritts said simply: “Mr Pearson is a natural who needs no guidance or direction.”[3]

His most visible cultural moment came when he was cast alongside Naomi Campbell, Linda Evangelista, Cindy Crawford, and Christy Turlington in George Michael’s Freedom! ‘90 music video, directed by a young David Fincher. The video became one of the defining documents of the supermodel era.[1]

Beyond the runway and campaign work, Pearson served as a contributing editor at British GQ and as consulting editor for Taschen’s Soul i-D, writing the foreword for that volume. He later co-founded Mr. Feel Good, a lifestyle, culture, and personal development digital magazine aimed at men, with journalist Pete Samson and creative director Alison Edmond — who is also his wife of more than twenty years.[5]

Recognition

Pearson appears on the L’Uomo Vogue Top 25 Male Models Ever list (Yahoo! Shine, 22 August 2012).[6]

The Sunday Times (UK) named him “The World’s First Male Supermodel.” Interview magazine called him an “Industry Legend,” and AnOther Magazine described him as the most “Iconic Male Model of All Time.”[2]

He is ranked on the Male Model Index, positioned among models whose careers function as reference standards within the global commercial advertising system.[7]

In 2026, the independent editorial archive MaleIconic — The 100 Greatest Male Models of All Time — recognized Pearson as one of the 100 most significant male models in history.[8]

Legacy

What makes Pearson’s career structurally unusual is its duration. Most male models of the 1990s supermodel era had careers measured in a handful of years; Pearson worked continuously at the top level for more than three decades, appearing in American GQ across thirty years while also maintaining an active commercial client list. The Rich Roll Podcast, which featured him in 2023, called him “the world’s first male supermodel” and noted his focus on healthy masculinity, fatherhood, and finding purpose in aging — themes that now define much of his public work through Mr. Feel Good.[9]

He lives in Los Angeles with his wife Alison Edmond and their three children.
