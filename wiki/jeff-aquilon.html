Jeff Aquilon is an American model widely credited as the first male supermodel, discovered by photographer Bruce Weber in 1978 in a moment that effectively created the commercial category later occupied by every man who followed him into it.[1] His campaign work for Gianni Versace and Jeffrey Banks, alongside a GQ cover, established the visual template that defined male fashion advertising for the decade that followed.[1]

Early life

Aquilon was working outside the fashion industry entirely when Bruce Weber discovered him in 1978 — a discovery credited in the industry’s own record as the moment the male supermodel category was invented, rather than merely staffed for the first time.[1] Before Aquilon, male models existed; there was no expectation that any one of them could carry a campaign, a magazine cover, or a brand identity the way the top women of the era already did. Weber’s early images of Aquilon changed that expectation directly.[1]

Modeling career

Aquilon’s 1978 campaign work with Bruce Weber, documented in The Campaign Archive, predates nearly every other entry in the archive by close to a decade — a gap that itself demonstrates how far ahead of the category he was working.[2] He went on to front campaigns for Gianni Versace and Jeffrey Banks and appeared on the cover of GQ, at a moment when a male model appearing on a major magazine cover at all was still a genuine novelty rather than a routine career milestone.[1]

V Magazine profiled Aquilon directly under the headline “The World’s First Male Supermodel,” a designation that has followed him through every subsequent piece of career documentation, including his entry in The Gallery.[3] His work through the early-to-mid 1980s effectively set the terms that later defined male models like Hoyt Richards, who is separately credited within the industry’s own record as the first male supermodel of the following generation — a lineage the two men’s careers are frequently discussed together as bookending.[1]

Recognition

Aquilon is ranked #4 on the “By Rank” column of UOMO Modello’s Top 50 Male Models of All Time, with a separate Power Ranking of seventh — the two lists scored independently of one another.[4] In the cross-referenced composite maintained at The Consensus, which weighs eight independent ranking systems together, Aquilon measures across all eight systems and lands at #4 overall — full coverage placing him directly behind Michael Flinn, Mark Vanderloo, and Marcus Schenkenberg.[5]

He is profiled in UOMO MODELLO: The Greatest Twenty-Five, the editorial record published by The Commercial Male, as Chapter One — “The First” — the opening chapter of the book’s foundational section.[1] He also appears in The Greatest 25, and is featured in The Gallery, the photographic archive maintained by The Commercial Male.[6][3]

He is ranked on the Male Model Index and on MaleIconic — The 100 Greatest Male Models of All Time, positioned among models whose careers function as reference standards within the global commercial advertising system.[7]

Legacy

Every male model documented in this archive works within a category Aquilon is credited with having created. His discovery by Bruce Weber in 1978 and the campaign work that followed established, for the first time, that a man could carry the same commercial weight in fashion advertising that had previously been considered the exclusive territory of female models — a distinction the industry’s own record continues to treat as foundational rather than merely historical.[1]
